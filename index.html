The software design process is the complete process of planning, analyzing, designing, implementing, testing, and maintaining a software program. The exact cycle of the software development process depends on the development model the developer chooses to use; let us take the waterfall model as an example. Programs start from the planning and analysis stage, where we set the requirements for the program and plan the program's basic structure. After the planning and analysis comes the implementation stage; this stage usually takes the most time, due to which some people see the implementation as the whole software design process, which is invalid. After the implementation stage, there are testing and maintaining stages, which are equally crucial, if not more crucial, than the previous stages. During the testing stage, developers build test cases to try and find errors in the program; this often needs to be clarified with debugging, which it is not. After satisfactory testing, the final task is maintaining the software, keeping it up-to-date, adding new features, and keeping it up to the latest standards. This process was an example of only one design model; many others have different advantages and disadvantages.

The process of software design is crucial to the lifestyle of almost everyone in the world. Without the help of the complete software design process, which includes design, implementing testing, and maintaining stages, developing a program would be extremely difficult or impossible. Without it, there would be no bug-free programs. Programs would be half-baked or non-functional. Programs crucial to people, such as Excel, Word, PowerPoint, and countless others, would not be here without the software design process. As previously mentioned, an example of well-designed software that many people have used is the complete Microsoft Office suite; the tasks it performs are pretty simple at times, however simple. Intuitive program is at the heart of many people's lifestyle. Its elegant and intuitive design is why many people use it daily, from writing academic essays to storing aircraft arrival and departure times; this software is used all over because of its openness, quality design, and an unbelievable amount of backward compatibility going back to 2008 and before. These are some reasons why the Microsoft Office suite is seen as one of the best-designed software of today's time.

Empathy comprises three elements: culture, emotions, and context.

Emotions are feelings experienced by individuals. Factors influence these feelings and are unique to each person. Businesses need to consider each individual's emotions as they can significantly impact their decision-making process.
Culture refers to the values that can influence how individuals make decisions. Businesses must be mindful of their importance as they often play a role in shaping consumer choices.
Context is the situational factors around the person that can influence their decision-making process.

Businesses should tailor their approach based on factors to foster a sense of loyalty among consumers. Businesses should consider their emotions, culture, and context when interacting with customers.

Prototyping in software design is a crucial stage where software developers can create a simplified and functional program model and present it to stakeholders before finalizing the development of the program. The primary purpose of prototyping is to try out various design options and test the usability of the software. Moreover, it is essential for ensuring an intuitive user interface, meeting requirements, exploring potential solutions to a problem and helping identify if a proposed structure fits the user's work model.

The three key benefits of using prototypes in the software development process are:

1. Enhance System usability; by allowing stakeholders/users to interact with a rough representation of the software, designers can use their feedback and fix problems in real-time.

2. User needs are considered in the design process; prototypes ensure that user's needs are thoroughly evaluated and met in the design process; this helps the designers to fine-tune features according to the users' needs.

3. Improve design quality: Through the process of prototyping, designers can identify and fix potential design flaws or related issues early in the development process, which leads to a more polished product.

Paper Prototyping steps: there are three main steps in paper prototyping: Determine needs, Build a prototype and evaluate the prototype, which are further divided into:

1. Choose the most essential user; in this step, designers choose the types of users they are targeting.
2. Determine some typical tasks; in this step, designers identify the functions that a user may need to perform with or on the program.
3. Make screenshots or sketches; in this step, designers make a rough sketch of the prototype to visualize items.
4. Perform a usability test; in this step, designers perform a test of their prototype with their targeted users to test how intuitive and functional is the software

Confirmation - requiring verification of actions before they are performed;
In the process of software design, this design principle states the importance of prompting users to confirm crucial actions to prevent accidental or nonreversible actions.

The design principle of confirmation is crucial in software design to prevent accidental actions that could lead a user to frustration or potential data loss; it acts as a safeguard, giving users the option to reflect on the decisions they are about to make, which leads to a more user-friendly and error free experience.

For example, let us consider an online shopping platform like Amazon, where users can remove items from their carts. A well-designed confirmation dialog appears when the user interacts with the delete button, making sure that the action that the user is about to perform is intentional; this prevents users from accidentally removing items from their carts and protects against potential frustration.

For another example, let's consider Gmail; when a user tries to select multiple emails at the same time and moves them to the trash section or presses the delete button, Gmail displays a confirmation message that asks the user to confirm the action they are about to perform. This additional step helps to prevent the accidental removal of important emails and enables an extra layer of security for the users. This confirmation design enhances the user experience by reducing the risk of frustration and unintended consequences.

Deceptive or dark patterns are design strategies or patterns that intentionally deceive or manipulate users into taking actions they might not have originally wanted to.

One such Deceptive pattern is "Confirmshaming." This design technique intentionally manipulates users by Using misleading patterns. Websites or apps frequently make users feel miserable about not using the provided service or feature by presenting opt-out button labels in an insulting or humiliating way.

The dark pattern of Confirmshaming explicitly violates the ACM Code of Ethics listed below:

1.1 Contribute to society and human well-being, acknowledging that all people are stakeholders in computing: Confirmshaming prioritizes achieving outcomes for the designer or company instead of contributing to society and human well-being, potentially leading to user frustration, dissatisfaction, and a negative impact on the broader societal perception of technology. Which directly undermines this principle.

1.2 Avoid harm: The technique of confirmshaming can lead to outcomes not in the users' best interest by coercing individuals into making decisions they might not have chosen otherwise. Doing this can result in harm on various levels, including emotional distress, dissatisfaction, or unintended consequences, as the users are compelled to take actions they may regret later. Due to these reasons, confirmshaming violates this principle.

1.3 Be honest and trustworthy: Confirmshaming uses deceptive tactics that violate the honesty and trustworthiness principle. The technique creates a misleading environment, giving users a false impression of the consequences or outcomes of their choices.
